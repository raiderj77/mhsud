# AUDIT-C Scoring in Primary Care: What Score Counts as a Positive Screen?

**Short answer:** The AUDIT-C is a validated 3-item alcohol screening tool. A score of 4 or higher is the standard positive screen threshold for men, and 3 or higher for women, according to NIAAA and SAMHSA guidance. These cutoffs flag likely unhealthy alcohol use and prompt a clinician to follow up, but a score alone does not constitute a diagnosis.

---

## What Is the AUDIT-C and Where Does It Come From?

**The AUDIT-C is a shortened version of the full 10-item Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test (AUDIT), developed by the World Health Organization. It uses only the first three AUDIT questions, which cover drinking frequency, typical quantity, and heavy episodic drinking. It was validated specifically for primary care settings where time is limited.**

The full AUDIT was developed by the WHO and published by Saunders and colleagues in 1993 as a brief, structured way to identify hazardous and harmful drinking in general health settings. The AUDIT-C was later derived and validated by Bush and colleagues in a 1998 study published in the Archives of Internal Medicine. That validation work showed the three-item version retained strong sensitivity for detecting unhealthy alcohol use while cutting administration time to under a minute.

Each of the three AUDIT-C questions is scored on a 0 to 4 scale, giving a total possible score of 0 to 12. The questions ask how often a person drinks, how many drinks they have on a typical drinking day, and how often they have six or more drinks on one occasion. Higher scores reflect more frequent and heavier drinking patterns.

---

## How Is the AUDIT-C Scored?

**Each of the three items is scored from 0 to 4, producing a total range of 0 to 12. The scoring weights differ slightly by question. A score of 0 means no alcohol use in the past year. Scores climb as frequency and quantity increase. The total is the sum of all three item scores.**

Question 1 asks about drinking frequency. Answers range from "never" (0 points) to "four or more times a week" (4 points). Question 2 asks about typical drinks per drinking day. Answers range from "1 or 2" (0 points) to "10 or more" (4 points). Question 3 asks how often the person has six or more drinks on one occasion, ranging from "never" (0 points) to "daily or almost daily" (4 points).

One practical note for counselors and care teams: the scoring on Question 2 is not a simple linear scale. A response of "3 or 4 drinks" scores 1 point, "5 or 6 drinks" scores 2 points, "7 to 9 drinks" scores 3 points, and "10 or more" scores 4 points. Misscoring this item is a common source of error in busy clinical settings, so it is worth double-checking against the official AUDIT-C scoring card.

---

## What Score Is Considered a Positive Screen?

**NIAAA and SAMHSA both support sex-specific thresholds. A score of 3 or higher is a positive screen for women. A score of 4 or higher is a positive screen for men. These cutoffs are not diagnostic. They indicate that further assessment by a licensed provider is warranted.**

The sex-specific thresholds exist because research, including the original Bush et al. validation work and subsequent studies cited by NIAAA, consistently shows that women experience alcohol-related harms at lower consumption levels than men. Using a single cutoff for everyone would miss a meaningful portion of women with unhealthy alcohol use.

SAMHSA's Treatment Improvement Protocol (TIP) 24, "A Guide to Substance Abuse Services for Primary Care Clinicians," reinforces these thresholds and frames a positive AUDIT-C as a signal to move into brief intervention or further structured assessment, not as a standalone finding. A clinician would typically follow a positive screen with the full AUDIT or a structured clinical interview to better characterize the pattern and severity of use.

It is also worth noting that some health systems, including the Veterans Health Administration, have used a threshold of 3 or higher for all patients regardless of sex. Clinicians should follow the protocol adopted by their specific practice setting.

---

## Does a Positive AUDIT-C Score Mean Someone Has Alcohol Use Disorder?

**No. A positive AUDIT-C screen means the person's reported drinking pattern falls above the threshold for unhealthy alcohol use. It does not confirm a diagnosis of Alcohol Use Disorder (AUD). Only a licensed provider conducting a full clinical assessment can make that determination, using criteria from the DSM-5-TR.**

The DSM-5-TR defines AUD as a problematic pattern of alcohol use leading to clinically significant impairment or distress, with severity rated as mild, moderate, or severe based on the number of criteria met. The AUDIT-C does not assess tolerance, withdrawal, loss of control, or the other diagnostic criteria required for an AUD diagnosis.

What the AUDIT-C does well is identify hazardous drinking, meaning a pattern that increases the risk of harm, even when formal AUD criteria are not yet met. NIAAA defines hazardous drinking for men as more than 4 drinks on any day or more than 14 drinks per week, and for women as more than 3 drinks on any day or more than 7 drinks per week. A positive AUDIT-C screen is often consistent with drinking at or above those levels, but a clinician would need to confirm that through direct conversation and further assessment.

## What Should Happen Clinically After a Positive Screen?

**A positive AUDIT-C screen should prompt a brief intervention or a referral for further assessment, depending on the clinical setting and the provider's scope. SAMHSA's Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment (SBIRT) model provides a structured framework for what comes next.**

SBIRT, which SAMHSA has supported and disseminated extensively, outlines three response levels. A brief intervention, typically 5 to 15 minutes, is appropriate for patients whose screen suggests hazardous or harmful use without clear dependence. A brief treatment, involving a few structured sessions, fits patients with moderate-risk patterns. Referral to specialty treatment is indicated when the screen and follow-up conversation suggest a more severe or complex presentation.

For patients whose AUDIT-C score and follow-up conversation suggest possible moderate to severe AUD, a referral to a licensed clinician for full evaluation is appropriate. That evaluation may include consideration of medically managed withdrawal, and a prescriber may consider options such as naltrexone, acamprosate, disulfiram, buprenorphine for co-occurring opioid use disorder, or methadone through an opioid treatment program. Those decisions belong to the prescriber, not the screening tool.

---

## Are There Limitations to the AUDIT-C That Clinicians Should Know?

**Yes. The AUDIT-C performs well as a screener but has known limitations. It relies entirely on self-report, it does not capture binge drinking patterns in people who drink infrequently, and it was originally validated primarily in male veteran populations. Clinicians should interpret scores in context.**

The Bush et al. 1998 validation study was conducted largely with male veterans, which is a specific population. Subsequent validation work has extended the tool to broader groups, but clinicians working with adolescents, older adults, or pregnant patients should be aware that standard adult thresholds may not apply directly. NIAAA guidance recommends that any alcohol use during pregnancy be treated as a concern warranting clinical conversation, regardless of AUDIT-C score.

Self-report bias is a real factor. Patients may underreport drinking due to stigma, fear of judgment, or simply not tracking their consumption accurately. A positive screen is meaningful, but a negative screen in a patient with other clinical indicators of alcohol-related harm should not be treated as definitive reassurance. The AUDIT-C is a starting point, not a conclusion.

## Frequently asked questions

### Q: What score on a standard screening tool should prompt a referral rather than a brief intervention?

A: Most validated tools use built-in severity thresholds. On the AUDIT, a score of 20 or higher suggests likely dependence and warrants referral to a specialist rather than brief intervention alone. On the DAST-10, a score of 6 or above signals high risk and should trigger the same step. Brief intervention is appropriate for moderate scores when no safety concerns, withdrawal risk, or co-occurring psychiatric symptoms are present.

---

### Q: Can these screening tools be used to diagnose a substance use disorder?

A: No. Screening tools identify risk level and flag individuals who may need further assessment. They are not diagnostic instruments. A formal SUD diagnosis requires a comprehensive clinical evaluation conducted by a licensed professional using DSM-5 criteria. Treating a screening score as a diagnosis is one of the most common misuses of these tools and can lead to under-treatment or inappropriate placement decisions.

---

### Q: Which populations are these tools not validated for?

A: Most widely used tools, including the AUDIT and DAST-10, were validated primarily on adult populations in outpatient or primary care settings. They have limited validation for adolescents, pregnant individuals, older adults, and people with significant cognitive impairment. For adolescents, the CRAFFT is the preferred option. Always check the original validation studies before applying any tool to a population outside its intended scope.

---

### Q: How often should a clinician re-administer a screening tool with an existing client?

A: There is no universal standard, but routine re-screening at meaningful intervals makes clinical sense. Many programs re-screen at intake, at 30 days, and at any significant change in status such as a relapse, new prescription, or life stressor. Re-screening is not a substitute for ongoing clinical conversation. It works best as a structured data point alongside direct assessment, not as a standalone check-in method.

---

### Q: What is the most common way clinicians misuse brief screening tools?

A: The most frequent misuse is administering a tool and then failing to act on the results in a structured way. A score means little if it does not lead to a documented clinical response, whether that is brief intervention, referral, or watchful monitoring with a follow-up plan. Screening without a clear protocol for what happens next reduces the tool to a paperwork exercise and misses the clinical opportunity it was designed to create.

---

### Q: Do self-report screening tools produce accurate results if a client is minimizing use?

A: Self-report tools depend on honest disclosure, which is a real limitation. Research shows they still perform reasonably well at the population level, but individual results can be skewed by stigma, legal concerns, or denial. Clinicians should treat a low score with clinical skepticism when behavioral observations, collateral information, or lab results suggest otherwise. The screening score is one data point, not the final word on a client's substance use picture.

---

### Q: Is a CADC-II qualified to interpret these tools and recommend next steps?

A: Yes, within scope. A CADC-II credential covers screening, brief intervention, and referral to treatment, commonly called SBIRT. That includes administering validated tools, interpreting scores within their intended framework, delivering brief motivational interventions for moderate-risk results, and making referrals when severity exceeds the scope of counseling-level care. What falls outside scope is formal diagnosis, prescribing, or managing medical withdrawal, which require licensure at a higher clinical or medical level.
