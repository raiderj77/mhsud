# How Does the CRAFFT Screening Tool Work for Adolescent Substance Use?

**Short answer:** The CRAFFT is a validated, six-question screening tool designed to identify substance use risk in adolescents ages 12 to 21. Developed at Boston Children's Hospital, it uses a simple scoring system to flag whether a young person needs brief counseling, further assessment, or referral to a specialist. A score of 2 or higher suggests a significant concern.

---

## What Is the CRAFFT and Who Developed It?

**The CRAFFT was developed by Dr. John R. Knight and colleagues at Boston Children's Hospital's Center for Adolescent Substance Abuse Research (CeASAR). It was first validated in a 1999 study published in the *Archives of Pediatrics and Adolescent Medicine*. The acronym stands for the six key questions in the tool's second part.**

The name itself is a mnemonic. Each letter represents a question: Car, Relax, Alone, Forget, Friends, Trouble. That structure makes it easy for clinicians and counselors to remember the items without consulting a reference sheet, which matters in busy primary care or school-based settings.

The CRAFFT was designed specifically because general adult screening tools, like the CAGE or AUDIT, were not validated for adolescent populations. Adolescent substance use patterns, risk factors, and developmental contexts differ meaningfully from adult patterns. SAMHSA recognizes the CRAFFT as an appropriate screening instrument for adolescents in its Screening, Brief Intervention, and Referral to Treatment (SBIRT) framework.

---

## How Is the CRAFFT Structured and Scored?

**The CRAFFT has two parts. Part A asks three yes/no questions about alcohol, marijuana, and other substance use in the past 12 months. If the answer to all three is "no," only one additional question is asked. If any answer is "yes," the full six-question Part B is administered. Each "yes" in Part B scores one point.**

Part A functions as a gateway. It asks whether the adolescent has used alcohol, marijuana, or any other substance in the past year. A "no" to all three moves the clinician to ask only the Car question from Part B, which screens for riding with an impaired driver, one of the highest-risk behaviors even among teens who do not personally use substances.

Part B scores from 0 to 6. According to the original validation research by Knight et al., a score of 2 or higher has demonstrated sensitivity and specificity for identifying a substance use disorder in adolescents. That does not mean a score of 2 confirms a diagnosis. It means validated screening tool results suggest a clinician should conduct a more thorough assessment. Only a licensed provider can confirm a diagnosis.

The CRAFFT 2.1, the current version, was updated to include cannabis vaping and e-cigarette use, reflecting changes in adolescent use patterns documented by NIDA's Monitoring the Future survey data.

---

## What Does Each CRAFFT Question Actually Ask?

**The six Part B questions cover the most clinically significant risk domains for adolescent substance use: impaired driving exposure, use as a coping mechanism, solitary use, blackouts or memory loss, peer pressure and social norms, and legal or school consequences. Together they build a picture of how substance use is functioning in a young person's life.**

Here is what each letter covers in plain terms:

**C (Car):** Have you ever ridden in a car driven by someone, including yourself, who was high or had been using alcohol or drugs? This question captures risk exposure regardless of the teen's own use status.

**R (Relax):** Do you ever use alcohol or drugs to relax, feel better about yourself, or fit in? This probes motivational patterns associated with higher-risk use, particularly use as emotional regulation.

**A (Alone):** Do you ever use alcohol or drugs while you are by yourself, alone? Solitary use is associated with more entrenched use patterns according to the CeASAR validation literature.

**F (Forget):** Do you ever forget things you did while using alcohol or drugs? This screens for blackouts and memory impairment, which can indicate heavier use.

**F (Friends):** Do your family or friends ever tell you that you should cut down on your drinking or drug use? Social feedback is a meaningful proxy indicator.

**T (Trouble):** Have you ever gotten into trouble while you were using alcohol or drugs? This captures consequences in school, legal, or interpersonal domains.

## Who Should Administer the CRAFFT and In What Settings?

**The CRAFFT is designed for use by a range of providers, including physicians, nurses, social workers, and certified substance use counselors, in settings like primary care offices, school-based health centers, emergency departments, and community health clinics. It takes approximately two to three minutes to complete.**

As a CADC-II, I use the CRAFFT in community-based settings as part of an initial intake process. It gives me a structured starting point for a conversation that might otherwise feel confrontational to a teenager. The tool is not a replacement for clinical judgment, but it creates a common language between the counselor and the young person.

SAMHSA's SBIRT model recommends universal screening for adolescents in primary care. The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP) also endorses annual substance use screening for adolescents as part of well-child visits, and the CRAFFT is one of the tools the AAP specifically references in its guidance.

Self-administered versions of the CRAFFT exist and have been validated. Some settings use a paper or tablet-based format, which research from CeASAR suggests may increase honest disclosure compared to face-to-face administration, particularly for sensitive items.

---

## What Do the Results Mean and What Happens Next?

**A score of 0 or 1 typically calls for positive reinforcement and brief education. A score of 2 or higher suggests the need for a brief intervention at minimum, and possibly a referral for a full substance use assessment by a licensed clinician. The CRAFFT result alone does not diagnose a substance use disorder.**

The CRAFFT fits within a tiered response model. Counselors and clinicians use the score to guide the next step, not to close a case.

For a score of 0 to 1 with no Part A use reported, a brief conversation about the risks of substance use and the importance of not riding with impaired drivers is appropriate. For a score of 2 or higher, a more structured brief intervention using a motivational interviewing approach is indicated. If the score is 4 or higher, or if the clinical picture suggests significant impairment, referral to a licensed clinician for a full diagnostic assessment is the appropriate step.

A licensed provider conducting that assessment would use criteria from the DSM-5-TR to determine whether a substance use disorder is present and at what severity. If a disorder is identified, treatment options a prescriber may consider include behavioral therapies and, in some cases, medications such as buprenorphine, naltrexone, or other options appropriate to the substance and the patient's age and medical history. Those decisions belong to the prescribing clinician, not the screening counselor.

---

## Are There Limitations to the CRAFFT That Counselors Should Know?

**The CRAFFT was validated primarily in English-speaking, clinic-based populations. It screens for risk but does not assess severity, co-occurring mental health conditions, or trauma history. Counselors should treat it as one piece of a broader intake process, not a standalone decision-making tool.**

The tool does not screen for specific substances beyond the initial three-category Part A questions. A young person using inhalants, prescription stimulants non-medically, or synthetic cannabinoids may score lower than their actual risk level warrants if those substances are not top of mind during the encounter.

Cultural and linguistic considerations matter. Translated versions exist, but counselors working with adolescents whose primary language is not English, or whose cultural context shapes how they understand questions about "trouble" or family feedback, should interpret results with that context in mind.

Co-occurring mental health conditions are common in adolescents with substance use concerns. NIDA's research consistently identifies high rates of co-occurring anxiety, depression, and trauma in this population. The CRAFFT does not screen for those conditions. A comprehensive intake should include attention to mental health history and, where indicated, referral to a licensed mental health provider.

## Frequently asked questions

### Q: What score on a standard SUD screening tool should prompt a referral rather than a brief intervention?

A: Most validated tools use built-in severity thresholds. On the AUDIT, a score of 20 or higher suggests likely alcohol dependence and warrants referral to a specialist rather than brief intervention alone. Scores of 16 to 19 indicate harmful use and typically call for brief counseling and closer monitoring. On the DAST-10, a score of 6 or above signals high-risk use requiring further assessment. Treat these cutoffs as clinical starting points, not final decisions. Always pair the score with a direct conversation about the client's current functioning and safety.

### Q: Can I use these screening tools with adolescents?

A: Most adult-normed tools, including the AUDIT and DAST-10, are not validated for clients under 18. For adolescent populations, use tools specifically designed for that age group, such as the CRAFFT. Applying adult screening instruments to younger clients can produce misleading scores and inappropriate clinical conclusions. Always check the validation population listed in a tool's original research before using it in a setting that serves minors.

### Q: What is the biggest misuse pattern clinicians make with SUD screening tools?

A: The most common misuse is treating a screening score as a diagnosis. Screening tools identify risk level and flag who needs further evaluation. They do not confirm a substance use disorder. A low score does not rule out a problem, and a high score does not confirm one. Clinicians sometimes stop at the score and skip the follow-up clinical interview, which is where accurate assessment actually happens.

### Q: How often should a screening tool be re-administered with an existing client?

A: Frequency depends on the clinical setting and the client's situation. In primary care, annual rescreening is a reasonable baseline for most adults. In SUD treatment settings, rescreening at each significant transition, such as a step-down in care level or a reported relapse, gives more useful information. Rescreening is not a substitute for ongoing clinical monitoring. Use it to track change over time and to support treatment planning conversations.

### Q: Are these tools validated for use with older adults?

A: Validation data for most common SUD screening tools skews toward working-age adults. Older adults metabolize substances differently, often take multiple medications, and may underreport use due to stigma. Standard cutoff scores can underestimate risk in this population. The AUDIT-C is sometimes used with older adults, but clinicians should apply lower thresholds and supplement screening with direct questions about medication interactions and functional changes related to substance use.

### Q: What are the core limitations of self-report screening tools?

A: Self-report tools depend on honest disclosure, which is affected by stigma, fear of consequences, and cognitive factors. They measure use patterns and associated problems but cannot account for biological tolerance, co-occurring mental health conditions, or social context without additional assessment. Results also reflect a single point in time. A screening tool gives you a signal worth investigating, not a complete clinical picture. It should always be one part of a broader intake process.

### Q: When is a brief intervention appropriate after a positive screen, and when is it not enough?

A: Brief intervention works best for clients in the mild-to-moderate risk range who have not developed significant dependence. It is designed to raise awareness and support behavior change in people who are not yet seeking formal treatment. If a client screens at a high severity level, shows signs of physical dependence, has a history of withdrawal complications, or has co-occurring psychiatric conditions, a brief intervention is not sufficient. Refer to a licensed treatment provider for a full assessment.
